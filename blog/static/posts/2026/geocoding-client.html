Problem #1: The 300-Meter Coordinate Drift

In China, consumer-facing maps don't use raw GPS coordinates (WGS-84). Instead, they use GCJ-02‚Äînicknamed "Mars Coordinates"‚Äîwhich applies a non-linear offset to every point. This isn't a simple translation; it's a deliberate obfuscation involving trigonometric functions that shift coordinates by 50‚Äì500 meters depending on location.

If your backend receives WGS-84 coordinates from GPS devices but passes them directly to a Chinese mapping API, your pins will land in the wrong place. Delivery zones break. Geofences fail. Users complain that "the map is wrong."

Problem #2: The TIME_WAIT Storm

Every naive geocoding request creates a new TCP connection:

connect() ‚Üí send HTTP request ‚Üí receive response ‚Üí close()

At 500 QPS, this creates 500 connections per second. Each closed connection enters TIME_WAIT state for 60 seconds (Linux default). Do the math: 500 √ó 60 = 30,000 sockets stuck in TIME_WAIT, competing for the ~28,000 ephemeral ports available on a typical system.
